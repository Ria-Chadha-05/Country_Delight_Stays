Country Delight Stays — User Study & Customer Understanding

Who are the users, why do they leave the city, how do they plan trips today, what frustrates them, and what's the opportunity? This document structures a set of testable hypotheses to answer those questions — it does not report findings from interviews or surveys that haven't happened yet.

● Hypothesis — reasoned, not measured ● Trend-grounded — connects to a cited secondary signal No market sizing, no TAM/SAM/SOM, no competitor benchmarking

⚠ Read this before anything else

No primary user research — interviews, surveys, or usability sessions — has been conducted for this document. A "User Study" title implies real users were studied; that hasn't happened yet. Rather than invent sample sizes, fake quotes, or fabricated frequency/severity data to make this look like completed research, this document is built and labeled as what it actually is: a structured hypothesis framework — reasoned from behavioural logic and a handful of legitimate secondary trend signals — designed to be tested by real interviews (Section 11), not treated as a finding.

01 — Executive Summary

What we're hypothesizing, and how confident we are in it

Major hypothesized patterns

Hyp. Trip planning for short/nature getaways is high-effort relative to the trip's own length — people spend disproportionate time comparing options for a 1–2 day trip. Trend Staycation and workcation demand is real and rising nationally (Statista, Forbes Advisor India).

Biggest hypothesized unmet need

Hyp. A trusted, short shortlist beats an exhaustive search — decision fatigue, not lack of options, is the likely core frustration.

Most promising segment (hypothesis)

Hyp. Families and young professionals seeking short, nature-based trips with minimal planning effort — same segment surfaced independently in the Market Assessment funnel, now approached from the behaviour side.

Key product opportunities

Hyp. Curated shortlists over search, one-day itineraries as a first-class format, and family/pet-friendly filtering as a trust signal, not just a feature.

Confidence level for this entire section

Low-to-moderate. These are informed hypotheses built from general travel-behaviour logic and a few cited secondary trends — not validated against actual Country Delight customers or Mumbai travelers. Treat this executive summary as a set of things to go test, not a set of conclusions.

02 — Research Methodology

What was actually done here — and what wasn't

Research objectives (proposed, not yet answered)

Who are the users? Why do they leave the city? How do they currently plan trips? What frustrates them? What's the improvement opportunity? This document proposes a framework to answer these — real research is still needed.

Interview methodology

None conducted. Section 11 proposes an interview guide for the next phase — semi-structured, 8–12 participants per hypothesized segment, mix of recent short-trip takers and non-travelers.

Secondary research used

A small number of cited, publicly available behavioural trend signals: staycation/workcation adoption (Statista), remote-work prevalence (Forbes Advisor India), corporate-outing packages as an existing booking channel (observed in market scan), digital-detox travel growth (Mordor Intelligence). These describe directional trends, not Country Delight's specific customers.

Sample demographics

Not applicable — no sample exists yet. A target sample profile for future research is proposed in Section 11 instead of a fabricated one here.

Research limitations

Everything in Sections 3–10 is inference, not measurement. Segment sizes, pain-point frequency, decision-driver rankings, and archetype behaviours are all untested. Real user variation could look meaningfully different from what's hypothesized here.

Confidence levels

Applied per-section throughout: Hypothesis for pure reasoning, Trend-grounded where a cited secondary source supports the direction (not the specific claim about Country Delight's customers).

03 — User Segments & Archetypes

Eight behavioural segments, seven motivational archetypes

Segments group people by how they likely behave day-to-day; archetypes describe the underlying motivation within a booking. Both are hypotheses to test in interviews, not measured findings. "Families" and "Parents with Children" were merged — same behavioural pattern.

Young Professionals

Frequency: Frequent, spontaneous
Duration: Day trip / 1 night
Budget: Mid-market
Planning style: Last-minute, social-media-driven
Pain point: Decision fatigue from too many options
Decision maker: Self or friend group

Couples

Frequency: Occasional, occasion-driven
Duration: 1–2 nights
Budget: Mid-to-premium
Planning style: Jointly researched, privacy-prioritized
Pain point: Crowded, non-private venues
Decision maker: Joint

Families / Parents with Kids

Frequency: Regular, school-holiday-aligned
Duration: Day trip to 2 nights
Budget: Mid-market
Planning style: Safety and hygiene-first research
Pain point: Uncertainty about child-friendliness
Decision maker: Parents, often mother-led research

Friend Groups

Frequency: Occasional, plan-dependent
Duration: 1–2 nights
Budget: Value-to-mid
Planning style: Group-chat consensus, slow to finalize
Pain point: Coordinating group availability and budget
Decision maker: Diffuse — group consensus

Pet Parents

Frequency: Constrained by pet logistics
Duration: Day trip preferred
Budget: Mid-market
Planning style: Heavy pre-filtering for pet policy
Pain point: Very few reliably pet-friendly options
Decision maker: Self, pet-need-driven

Corporate Teams

Frequency: Periodic, budget-cycle driven
Duration: Day trip / half-day
Budget: Set by admin/HR budget
Planning style: Admin-booked, package-based
Pain point: Needs group logistics handled end-to-end
Decision maker: HR/admin, not the attendees

Solo Travellers

Frequency: Irregular
Duration: Day trip to 1 night
Budget: Budget-to-mid
Planning style: Independent research, safety-conscious
Pain point: Safety and value perception travelling alone
Decision maker: Self

Senior Couples

Frequency: Occasional
Duration: 1–2 nights
Budget: Mid-to-premium
Planning style: Comfort and accessibility-first
Pain point: Unclear accessibility/mobility information
Decision maker: Joint

04 — Travel Behaviour

Hypothesized patterns, not measured frequencies

When they travel

Hyp. Weekends and long weekends dominate; monsoon and winter are likely seasonal peaks for nature destinations near Mumbai.

Who they travel with

Hyp. Mostly group travel (family or friends) rather than solo, given the destinations involved (farm/nature experiences skew social).

How far they travel

Hyp. Day-trip and short-getaway segments likely stay under 150km; distance tolerance probably rises with trip duration and group size.

Preferred transportation

Hyp. Self-drive or private cab dominates for family/couple segments; public transport more common for solo/budget-conscious segments.

Preferred accommodation

Hyp. For day trips, accommodation is often irrelevant — the "resort day-pass" model (pool/amenities without overnight stay) may matter more than lodging itself.

Preferred activities

Hyp. Passive relaxation (pool, food) and light nature activity (walks, farm visits) likely outweigh high-exertion adventure for the family/couple segments.

10 — Design Principles & Implications

What the research (hypotheses) implies for design

Trust before choice

Establish credibility before presenting options, not after.

Curate instead of listing

A short, vetted shortlist beats an exhaustive search every time decision fatigue is the real problem.

Experiences over accommodation

Lead with what someone will feel and do, not where they'll sleep.

Simplicity over abundance

More options isn't the same as more value — resist the urge to maximize catalog size.

Family-first design

Default to the more cautious, safety-conscious user; it rarely alienates anyone else.

Local authenticity

Real farms and real people, not staged or overly commercialized versions of "nature."

Minimal planning effort

Every additional decision the user has to make is a place they might abandon the flow.

11 — Research Appendix

What real research should look like next

Proposed interview guide

  • Tell me about the last time you took a short trip or day trip out of Mumbai. Walk me through it from the moment you decided to go.
  • How did you decide where to go? What almost made you choose somewhere else?
  • What was the most frustrating or time-consuming part of planning it?
  • If you have kids/pets, how did that change your options or decision?
  • Would you have paid more for someone to have already vetted the option for you? How much more?
  • What would make you trust a new brand enough to book a nature/farm experience through them?
  • [If mentioned spontaneously] Have you used Airbnb / StayVista / MakeMyTrip / similar for this kind of trip? What worked or didn't?

Note on the last question: per the brief's own guideline, competitor mentions should only be explored if participants raise them naturally — not prompted as a comparison exercise.

Proposed survey questions (for a larger-N validation pass)

  • How many day trips or short (1-2 night) trips have you taken in the last 12 months?
  • Rank these decision factors in order of importance: trust, price, distance, reviews, child/pet-friendliness, food quality.
  • On a 1-5 scale, how much effort does planning a short trip typically take you?
  • Would you pay a premium for a pre-vetted, curated experience vs. planning it yourself? (Yes / No / Depends on amount)

Affinity mapping (process, not fabricated output)

Once real interviews are conducted, responses should be affinity-mapped by clustering similar quotes/observations into emergent themes — the segments and archetypes in Section 3 should be treated as a starting hypothesis set to be revised, merged, or discarded based on what actually emerges, not confirmed by fitting real answers into these pre-existing boxes.

Open questions & research gaps

Do these 8 segments actually hold up?

They may collapse into fewer, or split further, once real interviews happen.

Is "willing to pay for curation" real?

The single most important untested claim in both this document and the Market Assessment.

What do people actually call this category?

We've been calling it "curated nature experiences" — untested whether that language means anything to a real user.

How does Country Delight's existing brand trust actually transfer (if at all)?

Assumed throughout prior work, never tested directly.

Future validation studies

Recommended sequence: (1) 8-12 qualitative interviews across the hypothesized segments to pressure-test whether they're real and whether the pain points/archetypes resonate; (2) a short quantitative survey to validate frequency and willingness-to-pay claims at scale; (3) a small pilot (referenced in the Market Assessment's Hypotheses section) to observe actual behaviour rather than stated preference.
